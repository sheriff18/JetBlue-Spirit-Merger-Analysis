2. MARKET DEFINITION - IS A ROUTE REALLY A MARKET?
============================================================================
  JetBlue argued markets are broad (all carriers, metro areas,
  connecting service). DOJ argued they are narrow. We run the
  hypothetical monopolist test on the flagged routes.

  Critical loss = t/(t+m). With a 5% SSNIP and a 25% margin,
  critical loss = 16.7%. If the predicted
  volume loss is smaller than that, the SSNIP is profitable and
  the candidate market is a relevant antitrust market.

3. UNILATERAL EFFECTS - DIVERSION AND GUPPI
============================================================================
  After the merger, JetBlue customers who would have switched to
  Spirit stay inside the merged firm. That recapture removes
  part of the cost of raising fares.

  We compute GUPPI two ways:
    (a) proportional (logit) diversion - the conservative baseline
    (b) ULCC-weighted diversion - DOJ's theory that low-fare
        customers substitute within the low-fare segment

  investigating and above ~10% as serious. The ULCC-weighted
  figures are higher by construction - that is DOJ's theory,
  and it is an ASSUMPTION about consumer behavior, not an
  estimate. The proportional numbers are the conservative floor.

5. SENSITIVITY - WHAT DOES THE CONCLUSION DEPEND ON?
============================================================================
  Every input above involved a judgment call. An opposing expert
  will probe each one, so we vary them ourselves.

      assumption  wtd avg fare change %
    margin = 15%                   4.37
    margin = 25%                   7.28
    margin = 35%                  10.19
 efficiency = 0%                   7.28
 efficiency = 3%                   6.03
 efficiency = 6%                   4.83
efficiency = 10%                   3.33

  Reading: the sign of the effect is robust across every
  assumption tested; only the magnitude moves. The efficiency
  level needed to offset the price rise entirely is the number
  the merging parties would have to prove - and under the
  Guidelines, efficiencies must be merger-specific and verified.

============================================================================
6. COMPARISON TO THE ACTUAL OUTCOME
============================================================================
  Judge Young blocked the merger on January 16, 2024, finding it
  would substantially lessen competition on concentrated overlap
  routes and eliminate Spirit as a low-fare competitor.

  This analysis reaches the same qualitative conclusion through the
  standard toolkit: overlap routes trigger the structural
  presumption, GUPPIs exceed agency thresholds, and simulation
  predicts fare increases that survive plausible efficiency credits.

  What this analysis does NOT capture, and the court weighed:
    * Spirit's role as an industry-wide price disciplinarian beyond
      its own routes (a dynamic effect, not a static one)
    * JetBlue's stated plan to reconfigure Spirit aircraft, removing
      seats - a capacity reduction, not just a pricing change
    * Entry and expansion by other ULCCs as a countervailing factor
    * The proposed divestitures

============================================================================
7. LIMITATIONS - STATED AFFIRMATIVELY
============================================================================
  1. Margin assumption. Airline variable margins are not observable
     in DB1B; 25% is an assumption that drives calibration. Section 5
     shows the conclusion's sensitivity to it.
  2. Logit demand. Imposes proportional diversion (IIA), the very
     assumption DOJ disputed. The simulation is therefore conservative
     relative to the government's theory.
  3. Static analysis. No entry, exit, or capacity response is modeled.
  4. Nonstop focus. Connecting itineraries are imperfect substitutes
     and are excluded from route shares.
  5. Synthetic panel. The shipped data is calibrated, not real -
     rebuild from DB1B before citing any figure.
